# LH Lessons #4 — The Most Important Skill for Awesome 1 on 1s, and How to Master It

*From Jason Evanish, Get Lighthouse*

---

In an ideal world, your team member would come prepared and lay out in perfect detail exactly what they need help with. They'd proactively ask you questions, reflect on themselves, and come to you with ideas to improve the team.

Unfortunately, this is not how management in the real world works.

Instead, you have to learn to **ask**.

By asking questions in 1 on 1s, it changes everything:

- Your team learns what are safe and good topics for these meetings
- You have a safe, private place to hear their thoughts without anyone judging or interrupting
- You will learn things you would never have otherwise
- You can build consensus, spot patterns, and see what your team needs

Yet, even understanding all of these benefits, it still requires significant skill to realize them.

---

## 1) Start with "What" and "How," Not "Why"

Two questions to avoid:

- **Yes or No questions** — removes all nuance and makes it easy for your team to avoid giving detail
- **Why questions** — tends to be accusatory and puts people on the defensive instantly, shutting down any hope of a productive discussion

Instead, ask questions that start with **"What..."** or **"How..."** — you get a full, detailed answer (you can't answer a What or How question with yes or no) without the baggage of "Why..."

**Example:** Which of these feels most comfortable to answer?

- "Why did you decide to do that?"
- "How did you arrive at that decision?"
- "What alternatives did you consider before making that choice?"

By switching to What or How, you can understand their decision-making process even in bad circumstances, without triggering defensiveness. It feels like a blameless post-mortem, not an accusation.

---

## 2) Bring Multiple of the Right Questions

**Ask the right questions.** Every time you broach a new topic in your 1 on 1s, you expand the coverage area. Once you talk about something, your team member knows it's a safe topic they can potentially bring up in the future.

**Have backup questions.** There are no perfect questions — only ones your team member responds well to, and ones that fall flat. This can vary from person to person and situation to situation. The real pros not only plan ahead on what topic they want to cover, but also prepare multiple questions on that topic. Always try to have 2–3 questions ready for any topic you want to explore.

### Example Topics for 1 on 1s

- **Building Rapport, Trust, and Psychological Safety** — foundational to your relationship (see Lesson 2)
- **Career Development and Goals** — critical for engagement and retaining your best people
- **Giving and Receiving Feedback** — 1 on 1s are a great time to give coaching and ask for feedback
- **Improving Your Team or Company** — your team sees processes and cultural habits up close; ask for their perspective
- **Checking in on Their Happiness** — especially after a big win, tough project, or around work anniversaries and other milestones

## 3) Practice Active Listening

You can bring all the right questions, ask them the right way, and still mess it up — by not truly listening.

> "The biggest thing I continue to learn is about really actively listening to people. Great leaders are great listeners. You have to ask a lot of questions and you have to really listen to the answers. Don't be thinking about the next question, and don't be thinking about what you're having for lunch. Really listen, because in every answer there are at least three more questions you want to be asking. I tell people to be comfortable with silence because after someone answers your question, something else is going to come out."
> — Melanie Whelan, former CEO of SoulCycle

**Fundamentals of listening:**

- **Focus** — turn off notifications, close Slack and email
- **Listen to their answer before doing anything else** — after they stop, often 3–5 seconds of silence will prompt them to add more before you ask a follow-up anyway
- **Write important things down** — "That sounds important to you. Let me write that down." They'll be grateful you noticed something they care about

**Active Listening levels** (from least to most effective):

- **Repeating** — parroting their exact words; shows you heard, but that's about it
- **Paraphrasing** — using different words to show some understanding
- **Reflecting** — repeating back the *ideas* they were trying to get across, not just the words; this ensures you understand the spirit and meaning

And it's okay to be wrong when you reflect! Ask them to clarify or correct anything you didn't get right — either they'll correct you so you truly understand, or they'll smile because you really get it.

## Being a Great Manager Is About Listening

To motivate and bring out the best in your team, you have to understand them and hear their perspectives. That means:

- Ask the right questions (using What and How)
- Bring multiple, similar versions of a question in case the first doesn't hit
- Become an Active Listener (ask follow-ups, clarify, then reflect back in your words)

---

## Your Action: Pick 2–3 Great Questions to Ask Your Team This Week

Find 2–3 questions on a topic you haven't had many conversations with your team about, and ask everyone. By asking everyone the same questions, you'll gain new, unbiased perspective from your whole team.

For any introverts on your team, consider giving them a heads-up ahead of time: *"In our next 1 on 1, I wanted to get your thoughts on [topic]. Here are some questions in case you want to think ahead."*

You may find there's something everyone on your team thinks you're missing, a meeting no one likes, or a great idea to improve how you work.

---

## Bonus: Be Strategic About What You Ask

Rather than simply picking questions that catch your eye, take a moment and reflect on what you think is the **biggest challenge or area for improvement** for you and your team right now. Then ask your team: Do they agree? Why or why not?

Preparing a few questions around your top challenge gets your team's perspective while avoiding group think in a public setting — and helps you understand how each person perceives the topic.
