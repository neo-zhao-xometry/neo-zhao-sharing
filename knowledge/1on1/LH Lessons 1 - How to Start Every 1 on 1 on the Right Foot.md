# LH Lessons #1 — How to Start Every 1 on 1 on the Right Foot

*From Jason Evanish, Get Lighthouse*

---

1 on 1s are incredibly valuable meetings. Ask any veteran, caring, effective leader and they'll tell you they swear by their importance:

- **Harvard Business Review:** "What happens when a manager doesn't meet with employees one-on-one at all? Employees in this situation are four times as likely to be disengaged as individual contributors as a whole, and are two times as likely to view leadership more unfavorably compared to those who meet with their managers regularly."
- **Adobe** saw a 30% reduction in voluntary turnover after implementing 1 on 1s.
- **GE** was able to "drive a five-fold productivity increase in the past 12 months."
- **Gallup:** "On average, only 15% of employees who work for a manager who does not meet with them regularly are engaged; managers who regularly meet with their employees almost tripled that level of engagement."

Yet, showing up in a room (or call) individually with one of your team members does not come close to ensuring you have a great, high value meeting. Nothing is more important than how you start.

---

## Set Expectations Before You Start

Do you remember back in school getting called to the principal's office? That sinking feeling of either being caught, or not knowing why you're in trouble, is never fun.

The same goes for an unexpected meeting with your boss. It's easy for one of your team members invited to a sudden 1 on 1 with you to think:

- Am I in trouble?
- Am I going to be fired?
- Will I get a bunch of unpleasant work dumped on me?
- Did I do something wrong?

To avoid panicking anyone on your team, and to have them come into the meeting with a great, positive frame of mind, you need to explicitly tell them about these meetings and your goals for them.

---

## A Slack/Email Is Worth 1,000 Scary Thoughts

A quick message to your team as you start this program is super important. They need to know why you're meeting with them and that you'll be trying new things — all with the goal to help them be at their best.

This removes any of their scary thoughts or dread of meeting with you. It also primes their mind to be open to what you will bring up.

This is really important whether you're starting these brand new, hitting the reset button on 1 on 1s that have gone stale, or are restarting them after previously giving up on them.

### What to tell them

A few key elements should go into telling your team about 1 on 1 meetings:

- **Why you're having the meeting** — most important, so they know it's a positive thing and have nothing to worry about heading into the first one.
- **That it's a meeting for and about them** — so much of work is "we need you to do this for the team/company"; these meetings are a breath of fresh air to help get their needs met.
- **When the meeting will be** — schedule the first meeting on your calendar right before or after you send the message, for some time in the next week or two.
- **Optional: Where to learn more** — if your team has no idea what 1 on 1s are, or have only experienced bad ones, it can be helpful to set expectations on what these will look like.

## Message Templates

### 1) If you're brand new to 1 on 1s

> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm going to start doing one on ones with you and other members of the team. This is to get more feedback from you as well as give you a place we can talk about anything that's bothering you, your career development, and a lot more.
>
> To start, we'll have the first one on [date]. We can then talk about a time that we can stick to regularly afterwards.
>
> If you want to learn more about what to expect, this post can help you: https://getlighthouse.com/blog/one-on-ones-employee-know/
>
> If you have any questions, please let me know.
>
> Thanks,
> [Your name]

### 2) If you're restarting 1 on 1s after giving up on them before

> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm going to start doing one on ones again with you and other members of the team. I recognize in the past the meetings weren't all they could be, so I want to try a new way of running the meetings. Now, the focus will be to get more feedback from you as well as give you a place we can talk about anything that's bothering you, your career development, and a lot more.
>
> I'm going to be putting a lot of effort into the meetings to show you how valuable they can be for both of us. All I ask in return is that you come with an open mind as we open up new topics and bring the focus to helping you thrive.
>
> To start, we'll have the first one on [date]. We can then talk about a time that we can stick to regularly afterwards.
>
> If you want to learn more about what to expect, this post can help you: https://getlighthouse.com/blog/one-on-ones-employee-know/
>
> If you have any questions, please let me know.
>
> Thanks,
> [Your name]

Note: in this situation, you'll have some awkwardness to push through. Some team members will be eager to see you trying again, while others will be skeptical. It may take time to get them to open up and trust your changes — hang in there.

### 3) If you're tuning up 1 on 1s you're currently having

> Hi [Name],
>
> In the coming weeks, I'm going to be experimenting with some new approaches to our 1 on 1s. My goal is to get more feedback from you as well as give you a place we can talk about anything that's bothering you, your career development, and a lot more.
>
> I'm going to be putting a lot of effort into the meetings to help make them more valuable for both of us. All I ask in return is that you come with an open mind as we open up new topics and bring the focus to helping you thrive.
>
> If you have any questions, please let me know.
>
> Thanks,
> [Your name]

## Send Them Individually

It would be easier to send one email with all employees on BCC or CC. However, the point of 1 on 1s is the personal relationship — it's worth the extra 5 minutes to send each message individually.

---

## A Note on Timing

The main focus is getting the first 1 on 1 on the calendar with everyone. Don't worry yet about long-term scheduling cadence (that's covered in Lesson 3). For now, just get the first meetings on the calendar with each of your team members — and pick times you know you can stick to. Canceling the first one would send a bad signal.

---

## Bonus: For Experienced Leaders

### 1) Start a peer 1 on 1 with a key stakeholder

Peer 1 on 1s are when you meet with someone not in your direct reporting structure. These meetings help keep everyone on the same page, improve cross-team collaboration, and build stronger relationships with the people and teams you depend on most.

- Identify one peer or stakeholder you need to work better with — maybe for a key ongoing project, or someone whose decisions impact your team.
- Ask to meet with them, and when it goes well, turn it into a semi-regular peer 1 on 1 (typically once every 2–8 weeks).
- Be sure to explain your reason for the meeting and bring a lot of value in meeting one.

### 2) Skip-level 1 on 1s (for senior leaders with managers reporting to you)

By meeting with people further down in your reporting structure, you can gain valuable insights into:

- How their manager is performing
- Where you may need to coach your managers more
- Front-line insights you don't see day to day
- How you can provide mentorship to more junior staff
- Where your messaging isn't getting through

**Before you start:** tell your managers you're planning to do this. Just like a new manager needs to explain why they're starting 1 on 1s, you definitely need to do the same before meeting with a manager's team. Let them know it's to help them — not to dig up dirt — and give them an idea of how long the round of skip levels will take.
