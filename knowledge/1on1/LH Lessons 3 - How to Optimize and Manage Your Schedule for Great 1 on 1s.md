# LH Lessons #3 — How to Optimize and Manage Your Schedule for Great 1 on 1s

*From Jason Evanish, Get Lighthouse*

---

One of the easiest ways for your 1 on 1s to not work out is to have issues with your schedule:

- Cancel too often? Your team won't take the meetings seriously when you finally do keep one.
- Distracted in your 1 on 1s? Your team members will check out quickly.
- Always coming late? That signals to your team member they aren't that important.
- Miss meeting with someone for a couple months because you got busy? You may be surprised how many problems built up — up to and including them deciding to leave.
- Overwhelmed by too many meetings? It's easy to burn out and not be at your best in any meeting.

Get it right, and you'll be able to apply everything else we're teaching you. Get it wrong, and nothing else will work.

---

## The TLDR Rules of Thumb

**By team size:**

- 5 or fewer team members → try to meet weekly
- 5–10 team members → try to meet every other week
- More than 10 team members → get help and get yourself under 10 (no one can effectively directly manage 10+ people)

**Meeting length:**

- Set aside an hour for each one — much easier to end early than to run long
- If you have a choice between 30 minutes weekly or 1 hour every other week, choose the larger block because of "zingers" (important issues that surface unexpectedly and need time to work through)

**Calendar fundamentals:**

- Make 1 on 1s a **set recurring meeting** — it won't happen consistently if it's not blocked off
- **Reschedule, don't cancel** — drag the recurring event to a new time that works for both of you (exceptions: vacations, extended leave, or extended sick time)
- If you can't be present mentally, reschedule — tell your team member why; they'll appreciate it over a checked-out manager half-listening
- If you're rescheduling often, change the scheduled time — consistency builds the relationship

## When to Meet More Often

Regardless of your default frequency, meet more often with:

### 1) Underperformers You Want to Turn Around

Coaching takes time. Frequent contact creates positive pressure — if they know they'll be meeting with you in a week or less, there's accountability to immediately work on what you discuss. Within a few weeks of weekly check-ins you could see significant improvement, or be building a clear case for HR.

**Bottom line:** Meet weekly (or possibly even twice a week) with any underperformers on your team.

### 2) Brand New Team Members

Regardless of their level, new hires need more support in their first 90 days. The sooner they're ramped up and operating at a high level, the better for you and your team.

**Bottom line:** Meet weekly with new hires until they're up and running at full speed and you trust they feel safe coming to you anytime they have questions (typically the first 90 days or so).

### 3) Low Task Relevant Maturity (TRM)

Andy Grove coined "Task Relevant Maturity" — how you calibrate hands-on management based on someone's *experience and skill with a specific task*, not a blanket generalization.

> "How often you monitor should not be based on what you believe your subordinate can do in general, but on his experience with a specific task and his prior performance with it — his task relevant maturity." — Andy Grove

Be more hands-on with even your long-standing team members when:

- They take on a new, important project that stretches their abilities
- Someone is promoted or takes on new key responsibilities
- You notice a team member seems uncertain, asking many more questions, or struggling

**Bottom line:** Decide how hands-on to be based on current tasks and responsibilities, not past work. Use your 1 on 1s to check in, coach, support, and determine whether they need more or less of this going forward.

---

## When You Can Meet Less Often

A word of caution: **Reducing 1 on 1 frequency is a veteran move.** You do not want any team member to feel less appreciated, nor miss when they really need help. If they tell you they want to meet more often, listen.

### 1) You Have a Strong, Long-Standing Relationship

If you've worked with someone for 5+ years and you both feel comfortable coming to the other with feedback, questions, or concerns at any time — you can reduce frequency. But never meet less than **once per month**.

**Bottom line:** Only reduce frequency if you know they'll come to you outside of a 1 on 1 if something is important or bothering them.

### 2) The Person Is Working Part Time

Part-time workers tend to have a narrower focus, less potential for drama, and less coaching needs. But don't eliminate 1 on 1s entirely.

**Bottom line:** Whatever fraction of full-time someone is working, roughly apply that as the degree to which you can reduce meeting frequency. (E.g., 10 hours/week → meeting once every 3–4 weeks likely works.)

### 3) Their Task Relevant Maturity Is High

If someone is working on something they're great at and doing really well, you can reduce frequency. But "hands off" does not mean "absentee landlord."

Before reducing frequency with someone due to high TRM, establish:

- Clear objectives and a way for them to keep you updated (so you can spot an issue early)
- That if they want to meet with you, you will find the time
- That this is temporary — it stands only as long as your schedule necessitates it, they don't need more help, and they're focused on projects with high TRM

**Bottom line:** You can meet less often with people doing things they're experienced and great at, as long as you have a good way to detect issues and step in as needed.

## Your Action: Audit Your Calendar

Review how frequently you're meeting with everyone.

**Meet more often if:**

- They're new to your team (less than 6 months)
- They are struggling or underperforming
- They are coming to you separately for a lot of questions and coaching

**Meet less often if:**

- You know them well, have worked great together for many years, and they'll come to you unprompted
- They're doing fantastic work and you have nothing to add or coach on
- Their work is consistent and requires little oversight
- They're working part time

Whatever you pick, explain it to your team members and get their thoughts — especially if you're reducing someone's frequency.

## Bonus: TRM Audit for the Pros

Make a table with 5 columns:

- Team member's name
- **TRM: High** → projects where you're confident in their skill, set expectations, and otherwise be hands-off
- **TRM: Medium** → projects where they're doing well, but you should still check in on how they feel and what they need
- **TRM: Low** → projects that need the most attention; plan to spend more time specifically on these

Once filled out, ask yourself:

- For TRM High: Have I set clear expectations and a time to check in?
- For TRM Medium: Have I made a note to ask them in my next 1 on 1 how they're doing?
- For TRM Low: As soon as I have time, am I checking in on the What, When, and How of the project?

In all cases, talk with them about how they feel about your evaluation — their perspective helps you better calibrate where they actually are.
