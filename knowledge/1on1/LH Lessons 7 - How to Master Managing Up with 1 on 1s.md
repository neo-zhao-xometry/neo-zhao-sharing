# LH Lessons #7 — How to Master Managing Up with 1 on 1s

*From Jason Evanish, Get Lighthouse*

---

There's another relationship just as important as your 1 on 1s with your team, if not more so: your relationship with your manager.

This is the person that has the largest say in your advancement, compensation, and general performance evaluation. They're also the person most likely to be able to help you when you feel stuck, need some guidance, or have a challenge with your team.

---

## Lesson #7: How to Master Managing Up through Better 1 on 1s with Your Manager

Too often, the story goes something like this:

You go to work at a company you really like. Your manager is pretty good and you're happy with your relationship. Then, as the team grows, your manager sees your potential and promotes you to a leadership role.

Unfortunately, the demands of your manager's new role now mean that right when you are facing a whole wave of new challenges, they're less available to you than ever. This leaves you feeling isolated, unsupported, and unprepared.

Without a better plan to manage up, this can lead to:

- Quitting in frustration, tired of being unsupported ("I never saw it coming!" says your manager)
- Having an adversarial relationship with your manager
- Deciding to succeed in spite of your manager — looking to transfer teams when other roles open up

All of these are sub-optimal outcomes. With an open line of communication, you can patch up a lot of issues and clear up a lot of misunderstandings.

---

## Embrace 1 on 1s with Your Manager as a Crucial Key to Your Relationship

Just like your 1 on 1s with your team members:

- It's **private**, making it safer to bring up even tough subjects.
- It's **recurring**, so you can regularly talk about issues without having to make it a big deal to get on their calendar.
- It's **frequent**, creating the opportunity to work through challenges and problems before they're massive fires.

If you're not having 1 on 1s with your manager, or they're too infrequent, that's the first thing you need to fix. Send your manager a note like:

> Hi [Name],
>
> I have a number of topics that have been coming up with my team, and areas I'd be interested in your feedback and coaching. In order to make progress on these regularly, I'd like to set up recurring 1 on 1s every week or two with you.
>
> I promise to come well prepared to make the most of our time together, and simply ask for your commitment to stick with these meetings on a recurring basis.
>
> Looking at our calendars, I see we're both open on [DATE] at [TIME]. Can we make that our first such meeting, or what time in the next week or two works best for you?
>
> Thanks,
> [Your name]

As Ben Horowitz writes in *The Hard Thing About Hard Things*, 1 on 1s are an essential part of the communication architecture of a company.

---

## Preparation Is Your Best Friend

Whatever effort you put into preparation for each 1 on 1 with your team, you should put *more* effort into this meeting. When you're preparing, think about 3 main categories:

- What does your manager want from you and your team?
- What do you need most from your manager?
- What does your team need that your manager can assist with?

---

## 1) What Does Your Manager Want from You and Your Team?

Finding out what they need is a key foundational step in your relationship. Some example questions:

1. Here are my top X priorities. Do these align with your priorities?
2. What are your priorities? What wins do you need?
3. What gives you confidence in the work of your team members?
4. How can I build more trust with you on the work I do?
5. What is most important to you to be up to date on for progress I'm making? Where do you trust my work and I can be more autonomous?
6. What causes you to feel stressed about my work? What gives you confidence?
7. What part of my work are you most comfortable and familiar with? What parts have you not done as much yourself?
8. What have your best team members done when working with you that you liked?
9. What have team members that frustrated you done or failed to do?

It will probably take a few months to ask all of these questions, but the effort will totally be worth it.

---

## 2) What Do You Need Most from Your Manager?

As executive coach Jerry Colonna puts it, the most important thing you can do is raise your self-awareness and ask: "How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?"

Take a hard look at your relationship with your manager. What's not working and how are you contributing to those issues?

- Not getting enough of their time and support? Have you asked for more help, and made it clear why you need more support?
- Feel like they don't care about you? Do you understand what's important to them and show empathy for them?
- Is your team being blocked or undermined by them? Do you know they're aware of this issue?

Some example questions:

1. If I need help with something, what's the best way to get your input or support?
2. What do you need to report on related to the work I do? How can I get it in a format that's easy for you?
3. I have [challenge] with my team — how would you handle that? What would you recommend?
4. I've been thinking about my career more and I'd like to do X. What areas do you think I need to work on to get there? How can we work together on those things?
5. How did you handle [situation/challenge] when you faced it when you had my role?

---

## 3) What Does Your Team Need That Your Manager Can Assist With?

It can be tempting to try to frame everything perfectly to your manager and never let them see any struggles. However, that's managing on hard mode.

In Wes Kao's framework on managing up:

> **Three tips to manage your boss:**
> - Offer solutions, not problems
> - Make requests, not complaints
> - Keep your boss in the loop

You are NOT coming to them with all your problems hoping they'll fix them. That's YOUR job. Instead, present the circumstances you have and get their perspective on how you're thinking about working on an issue, or make a specific ask they have the power to help you with. A few examples:

- Feel like your remote team is not collaborating as well as it should? Ask for budget and suggest things you could do at various budget levels to bring your team together more.
- Have a star performer you'd like to invest further in? Suggest ways to help them grow faster, and ask your manager about what projects and potential upcoming openings they should be in consideration for.
- Find out two of your team members have serious conflict? Explain the situation to your manager, lay out the resolutions you're considering, and ask for their feedback on which they'd recommend.
- Feel it may be time to let an underperformer go? Lay out what you've done to try to improve them and see if your manager agrees, or what they recommend you try.

In all of these cases, the goal is to surface issues and topics you think are worth their attention, presenting them as "here's the situation... and here's what I'm thinking of doing" — putting your manager in a healthy position of providing guidance, not expecting them to have all the answers for you.

---

## Your Action Today: Plan Out What You Need Most from Your Manager Right Now

Work through the 3 categories and bring 1–2 things for each of them:

- What does your manager want most from you and your team?
- What do you need most from your manager?
- What does your team need that your manager can assist with?

This will easily fill an hour-long 1 on 1 with your manager, and demonstrate to them these meetings are useful, important, and well worth having on a regular basis.

## Bonus for the Pros

If you already have an incredible relationship with your manager — congratulations! You are a true outlier. Take a minute this week to thank your manager for being this awesome. It is truly rare.

Now, flip this concept of managing up on yourself: **How can you make it easier and better for your team to manage up to YOU?**

Make a note to talk to your team about these key areas so you can mutually agree on ways they can better manage up to you:

- **Save your time** — Discuss places where you could use their help to make things easier for you.
- **Key goals and priorities** — Make sure your team understands those changes, and knows when to communicate things they're seeing to you.
- **When to come to you** — When is a problem important enough to raise with you, and how would you like them to do so?

By having an explicit discussion about these, you'll help ensure your team makes your life easier in places where you need it, and prevent problems from boiling over that you wish you'd known about sooner.

Also, when they properly manage up to you based on these things you discuss, be sure to praise them for it. That will ensure they keep doing it.
