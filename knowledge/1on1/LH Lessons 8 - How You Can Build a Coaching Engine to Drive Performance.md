# LH Lessons #8 — How You Can Build a Coaching Engine to Drive Performance

*From Jason Evanish, Get Lighthouse*

---

Being a leader today means embracing coaching as a core part of your responsibilities. To bring out everyone's best and deliver on the goals your company has for your team, coaching is a critical part of your job.

Despite this, many managers don't make time for it, and aren't even sure how to do it well. That's why we're going to dive deep this week.

---

## Lesson #8: Building a Coaching Engine to Bring out Your Team's Best

### Coaching Is a Key Part of Your Job as a Manager

On the surface, it may not seem like it matters that much — your team shows up, does their job, and you pay them. As long as they pass a minimum quality bar, it's okay, right?

Not quite.

**Not giving feedback creates management debt.** It's how small problems snowball into big issues.

What could be an easy, quick conversation about handling a customer issue differently, being more self-aware in a meeting, or working on tone in an email — can become a big problem when it happens over and over. Now coworkers are frustrated, or a customer is churning, or you have to mediate a blow-up.

Or you end up giving someone a low performance review, and they're totally shocked and upset: "Why did you wait to tell me until it affects my compensation?!"

Your team needs to know where they stand and how they can improve — for your sake and theirs. Focus on coaching and fixing problems when they're small, because it makes your team operate at a higher level.

Yet, feedback is not enough. People need praise, too.

---

## Praise Matters

Praise has a huge impact on your team members:

- A study shared in the **Harvard Business Review** found that managers with a **5:1 praise and positivity to criticism and negativity ratio** far outperformed other teams on financial performance, customer satisfaction ratings, and 360-degree feedback ratings.
- **Gallup** found that the statement "In the last seven days, I have received recognition or praise for doing good work" is responsible for a **10% to 20% difference in revenue and productivity.**
- Gallup also found that **employees who report not being adequately recognized at work are three times more likely to say they'll quit in the next year.**

---

## When Am I Supposed to Do This? Your 1 on 1s.

Your 1 on 1s are a massive Swiss Army Knife for working with your team — yet many managers have only ever thought to use the wine bottle opener.

**The perfect time for coaching** — 1 on 1s are ideal because they're:

- **Private** — even a touchy or difficult subject can be explored without embarrassment or feeling ganged up on. For introverts who are uncomfortable with public praise, this is the preferred way they receive it as well.
- **Focused** — 1 on 1s are for them, so it should be no surprise to them to talk about their performance, whether good or bad.
- **Already on your calendars** — it can be very scary to hear "I want to talk to you privately about something later today..." but if you simply add it as an agenda item in a 1 on 1, it's not nearly as big of a deal.
- **Easily followed up on** — their next 1 on 1 should only be a week or two away, making it easy to follow up on how they did.

## How to Give Praise That Motivates

Not all praise is created equal. Thumbs up, "good job," attaboy, and similar basic phrases don't count. In fact, they can hurt — we all know when praise rings hollow.

Remember 3 words to giving praise that really makes an impact:

### 1) Frequent

Praise is not remembered for very long, which means you have to keep giving it. That's why Gallup's study specifies "in the last **seven** days."

Science reinforces this: negative comments fuel the release of the stress hormone cortisol and have an effect on the human brain for 26 hours or more, while positive ones — which release the bonding and trust hormone oxytocin — last for a much shorter period of time. This means you need to make it a recurring habit to recognize good work and praise-worthy behaviors.

### 2) Specific

Praise only works if you tell them **why** you're praising them. And more importantly, if you tell them specifically what you liked, they're very likely to do more of it.

As Dale Carnegie recounts in *How to Win Friends and Influence People*, a manager nearly lost his best employee — a vital link to 21 staff members — until he took her aside and said:

> "Paulette, you must understand that I cannot accept your resignation. You mean a great deal to me and to this company, and you are as important to the success of this restaurant as I am."

He repeated this in front of the entire staff. Paulette withdrew her resignation, and he frequently reinforced this by expressing his appreciation specifically for what she did.

### 3) Strategic

To become a true master of praise, use it as a tool:

- **Get more of what you want** — praise specifically what you want to see more of
- **Encourage your team in areas you want them to improve** — recognize great actions to encourage those to keep happening

Examples of strategic praise:

- Praising the speed and friendliness with which a support person responded to and resolved a customer issue
- Recognizing how the whole team rallied when there was an outage, calling out what each person did to contribute
- Praising a marketing team member for doing great research for a blog post while needing very little guidance

## How to Give Feedback That Leads to Great Improvement

Remember 3 words for constructive feedback:

### 1) Prepare

The single most important thing you can do as a manager giving feedback is to be prepared. Before giving feedback, work on having answers to:

- When did this happen? What is the context?
- Is this an isolated event, or has it happened repeatedly?
- Why is this important? How does this affect others?
- What are specific examples of when this happens?
- What do they need to do differently going forward to address this?

### 2) Listen

This is the most counter-intuitive part. Before you give feedback, you need to step back and listen.

It's amazing how many times a fully planned feedback session gets reframed by some bit of context you would have missed:

- A death in the family they were mourning
- A severe criminal incident in their personal life
- Feeling completely exhausted and spread too thin on too many projects
- Long-term effects from illness giving them brain fog and decreased productivity

If you dive right into feedback, you'll never get that crucial context. Before diving in, start the meeting by asking how they're doing:

- How is [project you're going to give feedback on] going?
- How do you feel your workload is right now?
- What's been your main focus lately?
- How are you feeling overall right now?

### 3) Act

All the talk in the world doesn't mean anything if that's where it ends. To truly make your feedback effective, you need to make the next steps clear. Tell them exactly what should change:

- Are they going to spell check all their work before sending it out?
- Are they going to send you a draft to review earlier in a process? When exactly?
- Will they be apologizing to a colleague in writing for an incident?
- Will another team member be paired with them to help teach them? Who? When?
- Is there a specific document with instructions they should be referencing?

Whatever it is, the more specific you can make the next steps, the more likely it is to happen. And **put it in writing** — that way there's zero chance of confusion, misunderstanding, or excuses.

## Bringing It All Together

In a past job, a team member made a terrible hire. The number of problems the new hire caused before they were let go was stunning. I couldn't believe the person made it through the hiring process — until I discovered it wasn't followed.

I was upset and prepared to go over in great detail why we had the specific hiring process we did.

However, once I asked them how they were doing and what their focus had been on, it all became clear: the problem was merely a symptom of a much bigger issue — they felt overwhelmed and stretched too thin.

Instead of talking a ton about our hiring process, the rest of our discussion focused on adjusting their workload. By the end of the conversation, they were apologizing about the hire and not following our process before I even brought it up.

Best of all, by fixing the root problem I learned about in our conversation, we prevented many future problems — and I earned their respect by showing empathy for their situation instead of just unloading my frustrations. This person stayed with the company through selling that business, and we're still friends to this day.

If you want to give feedback that makes a difference and has a lasting impact, follow the **Prepare – Listen – Act** model.

## Your Action Today: Give 1 Piece of Coaching to Everyone on Your Team This Week

Your challenge is to give 1 piece of coaching to everyone on your team. It can be feedback or praise, depending on what feels best for each team member. Just remember:

- **For Praise:** Make it Frequent, Specific, and Strategic.
- **For Feedback:** Prepare, Listen, then Act.

And if you need a reminder to help you in your preparation for giving feedback, add the following to your private notes for that team member's 1 on 1:

> "Practice the Prepare-Listen-Act model: Prepare examples of the area of feedback. Listen to their situation and perspective, then make clear action items for both of you on what needs to happen next to bring change."

---

## Bonus for the Pros

Make your praise of your team members as strategic as possible — their most important work, what you value most, an area you're loving their improvement — and for the feedback, go the extra mile to review some of their work that you know they'll value hearing your perspective on.

Never underestimate the value of your attention to your team members.
