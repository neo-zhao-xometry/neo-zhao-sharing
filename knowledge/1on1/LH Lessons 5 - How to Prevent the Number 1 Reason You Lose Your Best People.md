# LH Lessons #5 — How to Prevent the #1 Reason You Lose Your Best People

*From Jason Evanish, Get Lighthouse*

---

When I lived in San Francisco, I was always surrounded by amazing, smart people working in tech. Yet, too often I'd have this conversation:

Me: "How's the new gig?"

Them: "Great! I love my team, the mission is cool, and the perks are really good."

Me: "What happened at your last job?"

Them: "Well, you know, they were great, too... but I felt like I was kind of stagnated there. I got bored, and this seemed too good an opportunity to pass up."

Famous last words — "...this seemed too good an opportunity to pass up."

As a manager, by the time they have that kind of opportunity, it's too late.

Most managers make 2 fatal mistakes:

- They assume if their team member generally liked their coworkers and the work, they were safe on retention.
- They assume a once-per-year check-in on their career is sufficient.

**50% of employees who accept a counteroffer leave within 12 months** — once people go through the trouble of interviewing, getting an offer, and working up the courage to tell you, they're too psychologically committed to the new role to stay.

---

## Show Me the Data

Work anniversaries are a huge spike in departures. Most people take time to reflect then — look back at what they're doing, think about where they're going. So is it any surprise many people make changes and take another job around this time?

The top perk Millennials have wanted at work is "Training and Development." And best-selling author Dan Pink mentions growth as one of his 3 pillars of motivating your workforce in his book *Drive*.

There's really no way around it: growth and a clear career path are things you need to both attract and retain good talent.

---

## The Secret to Career Growth: Your 1 on 1s

You don't need to talk about their career every week, but it's very helpful to check in about once a month. It also helps to break up your team members' goals in a way that you two can make some progress on their career together incrementally.

When you do that, they'll reflect on their work anniversary on all the progress they've made on their career.

---

## Applying This to Your 1 on 1s

There are two scenarios to handle:

1. **Starting out** — When you don't know their goals (or they don't), you have to first figure out what they want to work toward.
2. **Keeping momentum** — Once you know their goals, you have to help them make semi-regular progress to get there.

---

## 1) How to Talk About Their Career Goals

Some people will know exactly what they want. Others may be at a crossroads. To help them, try questions like:

- "What do you want to be doing in five years?" and work backward to what the path starting today needs to be.
- "Who do you look at and say, 'I want to be him/her someday?'" and work back on how to get into the kinds of roles they've had.
- "What job do you envision having after you leave [this company]?" — this admits the truth that no one works at a company for life anymore, and opens the door to opportunities in other roles they may not know about.
- "Imagine it's 10 years from now and there's a party celebrating you and your accomplishments... Who is there? What music is playing? And what is the accomplishment everyone is there to celebrate?"
- "What work are you doing here that you feel is most in line with your long-term goals?"
- "If you had millions of dollars, what would you do every day?" — this can reveal what parts of their job they get joy from regardless of compensation.
- "What are your superpowers? What powers would you like to develop?" — then use your knowledge and instincts to consider what career path may best utilize them.

This is a big deal for people and can take time to answer. Give them a heads-up ahead of time that you want to talk about their career — and be prepared for some people to want to think on it and add more a week or two later.

Also keep in mind that sometimes it's not the right time to talk about their career. Major life milestones, illness or crisis, or starting something brand new can all make career conversations premature. Simply tell them that when they're ready, you're here.

---

## 2) Keep Momentum and Make Progress Regularly

Once you know what their career goals are, it's only the beginning. Work with them on a plan. Break it down.

Ask yourself (and potentially them):

- What skills do they need to master to reach that goal? How can they work on these skills in their current role?
- What books, courses, seminars, and other training material can they consume?
- What projects should they get involved in (or potentially lead) to help their growth?
- What are the most important things you feel you would need to see from them to advocate for their promotion?
- What do you think are the biggest weaknesses they need to work on before they can be ready for promotion?
- What aspects of their career goal are very different from their job today? How can you help them experience or work on that in small ways?
- Who do you know that has the job they're interested in next? Can you introduce them for a conversation or potential mentorship?
- How can you leverage your training budget to invest in their growth? What ideas do they have?

If you have career laddering at your company, get a copy of the latest ladder for the area each of your team members is interested in — it's a great shortcut to answering many of the above questions.

### Make a Plan

List these things out and work with your team member on how you can chip away at them one at a time.

For example, this could be a simple timeline:

- **January:** Finish reviews, set goal, and break it down with them.
- **February:** Buy them a book on a skill area they need to work on. Talk about what they learned when they finish.
- **March:** Introduce them to a colleague who is great at a skill they're focused on.
- **April:** Buy them a course to go deeper on a topic they started learning from their new mentor.
- **May:** Get them involved in a project with some oversight that lets them practice their newly learned skills.

The best things you can do as a manager to support them:

- **Bring the structure** — Help craft the plan and evaluate their skills to head on that career path.
- **Provide budget** — Most companies have a learning budget for each team member. Find out what yours is, or decide what you'd personally invest.
- **Do the little things you can** — A good intro, the right project responsibilities, or sending something relevant their way can do wonders for their morale and progress.

---

## Your Action Today: Check in on All of Your Team's Careers

Add this to your 1 on 1 agenda for each of your team members. Give them a heads-up: "Hey — I'd like to talk about career goals in our next 1 on 1. If you can prepare some thoughts or questions, it will help us have a great discussion."

A plan doesn't have to be fancy. The bare essentials:

- Bite-size, manageable steps that can be achieved in a quarter or less each
- A due date for those you want them to focus on next, so you know when to check in
- A record of what's been done so you can easily look back at review, compensation, and promotion time

If you don't know your team's goals yet, all you need to accomplish this week is figure out their goals. Work on the plan in coming weeks and months.

---

## Bonus for the Pros

If you're a pro who already knows everyone's goals and has a plan for their growth, the best thing you can do now is make an investment in that growth.

Find out the budget each of your team members has and encourage them to think about how they'd like to spend it. Then make the process of investing as easy as possible. Books are only $10–$20, after all.
