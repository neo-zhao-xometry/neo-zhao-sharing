## Lesson 10: A Proven, Simple Principle to Motivate Your Team Members in Every 1:1

You as a manager have significant control over whether someone is happy or unhappy in their job.

While there are many things that can impact their morale, the feeling of **progress** on their work and things that are important to them is one of the largest drivers.

Harvard Business School professor and researcher Teresa Amabile's findings came from following 238 workers across over 12,000 surveys of their workdays. What she found should influence everything you do. As she wrote:

> "In a dramatic rebuttal to the commonplace claim that high pressure and fear spur achievement, we found that, at least in the realm of knowledge work, people are more creative and productive when their inner work lives are positive — when they feel happy, are intrinsically motivated by the work itself, and have positive perceptions of their colleagues and the organization."

But what does it mean to have a positive inner work life? That's where the real breakthrough insight came in:

> "Our hunt for inner work life triggers led us to the **progress principle**. When we compared our research participants' best and worst days (based on their overall mood, specific emotions, and motivation levels), we found that the most common event triggering a 'best day' was any progress in the work by the individual or the team. The most common event triggering a 'worst day' was a setback."

So helping your team be as creative and productive as possible comes down to making them feel progress regularly. And that's easier than you may think:

> "Even ordinary, incremental progress can increase people's engagement in the work and their happiness during the workday.
>
> ...a notable proportion (28%) of incidents that had a minor impact on the project had a major impact on people's feelings about it."

**Put simply: Small feelings of progress can have a BIG impact for your team.**

## The Dark Side of Progress: Learned Helplessness

The opposite of progress is a term you must know about as a manager: **Learned Helplessness**.

Psychology researcher Martin Seligman's studies showed that animals (including humans) were susceptible to giving up all hope when put in an environment where negative outcomes were beyond their control.

In Seligman's most notorious study, he gave dogs electric shocks:

- One group could stop the shocks by pushing a lever.
- The other group could not control the shocks.

Both groups were then put in a box they could easily jump out of and shocked again. While the first group jumped out of the box without issue, those who had been in an environment with no control did not jump to escape — they laid and took the shocks.

### A Lack of Progress for Too Long Causes Learned Helplessness

Ever been on a project that never ends? Ever have that job where you feel like nothing you do matters? How did you feel?

Chances are, it became harder and harder to get up for work each day. You probably started to feel burnt out. You may have even quit.

At Lighthouse, we added a Microsoft Teams integration at customer request. After some hesitation, we put one of our best engineers on the project. What was supposed to be a quick project ended up taking almost 6 months because:

- The Microsoft APIs required an entire separate app be built to create similar functionality.
- Documentation was spotty and often out of date.
- Multiple contacts at Microsoft sent us in circles.
- The approval process was brutal, with new testers who didn't follow basic instructions.

And the day it finished, the engineer quit, saying he was completely burnt out.

This taught me a very important lesson about burnout, learned helplessness, and the essential importance of making sure your team feels regular progress. Now, especially on long-term projects, we make sure to build in breaks. We celebrate wins along the way and plan mini-milestones.

## What Does Any of This Have to Do with 1 on 1s?

If you know that progress is important to your team, then your 1 on 1s are one of the single best places to make sure your team feels it regularly.

Think back to many of the lessons we've covered:

- **Check in on them** — How are they feeling? How are their projects going? Ask if they feel progress.
- **Celebrate wins** — Like we talked about in Lesson 8, give your team some praise to help deepen those feelings of positive progress.
- **Work on their career** — Remember Lesson 5? Your 1 on 1s are the perfect time to not only talk, but also set an action plan for regular, small progress on their career.
- **Remove blockers** — Is Learned Helplessness starting to creep in? Use your curiosity (Lesson 4) and ask about it, then do something about it together. Don't find out when they resign.
- **Redirect their attention** — If they're stuck on one thing, use your 1 on 1s to find other things they can do in the meantime where they can feel progress.
- **Talk about progress** — Sometimes people cannot see the forest for the trees. If you see progress happening that they don't recognize, tell them!

## Action Items for the Win

Every good 1 on 1 should end the same way: **Establish clear next steps on what you both will do before next time you meet.**

This is powerful for a few reasons:

- **It turns talk into action.** Clear next steps turn a performance discussion into real improvement.
- **It avoids broken records.** You don't want to talk about the same thing every week with nothing changing. Setting action items ensures that won't happen.
- **It sets up a regular feeling of progress.** These little action items, small enough that you can both do them in the next week or two before your next 1 on 1, means you create a feeling of progress every 1 on 1.

Remember: Prof. Amabile's research showed that even small progress could make a big difference — "incidents that had a minor impact on the project had a major impact on people's feelings about it." That means even small steps to improve a problem at work, one act towards their promotion a year or two away, or a small win on a project can boost their morale and motivation.

So remember every single 1 on 1, whether it was a really tough or really positive conversation, ask yourself:

> "What can we both do to make progress on what we talked about today?"

Then write it down, email/message it to both of you, and check back in on it next 1 on 1.

That's how you make your team feel progress regularly and keep them motivated.

## Your Action Today: Check in on the Feelings of Progress for Your Team Members

For whatever system you use for your 1 on 1, put this in your notes so you remember to ask about it.

Ask how they feel about their progress lately. Where do they feel stuck? How could you work together to unblock them?

You can copy this:

> "Are you happy with your progress here right now? In what ways, if any, do you feel stuck? Let's talk about this and see how we can work together on any ways you feel you lack enough progress."

When you hear the answers, really listen and understand them. Then try to find ways to take action and help them if they're particularly stuck, so they feel a little bit of progress right there in your 1 on 1.

## Bonus for the Pros: Reflect on the Progress of Your Team

The best managers are proactive, not reactive. That means anticipating problems and being on the lookout for when your team members may be in trouble.

Put simply:

- Make a list of your full team.
- Write out what the major projects are that each of them are working on. (If you don't know, find out!)
- Look at which ones seem to be progressing well, and which ones may be dragging on or stalling out.

Once you have this list, for anyone stuck on a long, painful, or bad project, make a note to proactively check in on them. See how they're feeling and proactively work with them to troubleshoot.

Instead of waiting for them to bring up problems with the project, ask them about it. Doing this will make sure you don't get blindsided like the Microsoft Teams story above.
