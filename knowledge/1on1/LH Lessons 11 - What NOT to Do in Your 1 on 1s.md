## Lesson 11: Bad Ideas to Avoid in Your 1 on 1s

Every single one of these mistakes has been something that multiple managers have told me they do, that Lighthouse customers have asked if it's okay to do, and that I've seen people suggest on LinkedIn, Twitter, or their blog.

There's always a bit of rationale why they seem like good ideas, but in practice, they're quite bad.

---

## Bad Idea #1: Using Your 1 on 1s for Status Updates

**The Reasoning:** "Finally! We have some time together. I don't know what they're up to lately, so let's use the meeting to get me up to speed on their work. I might hear about some blockers or issues at the same time, so this is a good use of my time, right?"

**The Result:** There's nothing safer than talking about project status updates in your 1 on 1s. It's also very easy to spend an hour just talking about projects — but that means you box out time for literally everything else we've covered in this program.

Status updates in 1 on 1s break the cardinal rule: **they're your team member's meeting.**

When you ask for status updates, that's making the meeting about YOU, the manager. That's how you end up blindsided by a giant problem exploding on your team, never hearing about issues, and losing good people because you never talked about their career.

Most importantly, it's terribly inefficient. If you have to ask each person on your team in a 1 on 1 for a status update, you have to have a conversation with everyone just to understand how things are going. Then you may find Joe needs to talk to Alma about something, and now you have to route information between people. And what you heard on Monday with Kelly may have changed by the time you talk to Nikhil on Wednesday.

### What to Do Instead

Get your status updates another way:

- **A weekly status update email from every team member** — allows you to reply with questions, and if you ask for them all on the same day, you get a clear snapshot all at once.
- **A project management tool** — there are great options for any department. Ask your team for input and choose together (this is a great area to get buy-in in your 1 on 1s).
- **Regular stand-ups or key project check-in meetings** — use stand-ups to keep everyone coordinated faster, fix problems, see who needs help, and make decisions as a group.
- **A daily stand-up tool** — for remote or async teams, a written standup that integrates with Slack or Teams can be just as effective.

Then, use your 1 on 1s for what we've talked about throughout this program: rapport and trust, career growth, buy-in for changes, feedback, and praise.

## Bad Idea #2: Using the 10/10/10 Rule for Your 1 on 1s

**The Reasoning:** "I'm a super efficient manager. I have 30-minute 1 on 1s with my team, and we evenly and fairly divide up the time using the 10/10/10 rule: 10 minutes for you, 10 minutes for me, and 10 minutes to talk about the future."

**The Result:** Every meeting is rushed, you rarely go past surface-level issues, and team members find it easier to bring little to nothing to the 1 on 1 than bother preparing. The manager thinks they're efficient and effective, but in reality, they have no connection to their team at all.

The 10/10/10 rule sounds great on paper. It's catchy and memorable, too. Yet, it is a recipe for disaster.

Have you ever had a big, thorny problem in your life you needed help with? Could you solve it in 10 minutes?

As Andy Grove wrote in *High Output Management*:

> "The subordinate must feel that there is enough time to broach and get into thorny issues.
>
> Look at it this way. If you had a big problem that you wanted to kick around with your supervisor — the person whose professional interest in the matter is second only to yours — would you want to bring it up in a meeting scheduled to last only fifteen minutes? You would not."

And rarely would any of us walk into a room and blurt out "I'm unhappy in my current role," "I have a personal issue you need to know about...," or "I'm very frustrated by..." When you only have 10 minutes, that won't happen easily.

### What to Do Instead

As we talked about in Lesson 3, ideally you'll set aside an **hour** for each of your 1 on 1s. It is so much easier to end early than it is to run long. This is especially important when one of you sometimes arrives late. Imagine following the 10/10/10 rule and one of you is 5 minutes late. Who loses half of their 10 minutes?

Setting aside an hour shatters the 10/10/10 rule. There have been many times I've had 1 on 1s where we spent close to the entire hour on a single topic — and that's totally fine and normal. Some weeks your team member has a big frustration and you need to listen to them pour their hearts out and work with them on solutions.

The best leaders understand that 1 on 1s are like a Swiss Army Knife — you end up needing a different tool for different situations.

## Bad Idea #3: Canceling Your 1 on 1s Frequently

**The Reasoning:** "I have a really busy schedule. Sometimes I just can't fit them in, so I cancel." Or: "If I don't see anything on their agenda, I cancel — the meeting must not be important to them." Or: "We've been working closely on a few projects, so there's no need for yet another meeting."

**The Result:** When you skip them often, you end up missing out on a lot of context. There's a lot that can happen when you don't make time to talk about what's important to them.

When you cancel, you send a bad signal to your team member. This is their one meeting with you that's for them, so when you cancel it, it can easily be perceived as they don't matter to you.

The impact of canceling:

- **It's hard to not agree.** The power dynamic means it's very hard to say no to your boss, even if they wish they could.
- **They'll resent it.** That same power dynamic means that while they likely agree to canceling, they will also resent you doing so.
- **You disrupt your meeting rhythm.** Good 1 on 1s build on each other week to week. Canceling halts that momentum.
- **Busy times are when they're needed most.** When you and your team are most stressed is the time 1 on 1s can make the biggest difference — they can be a crucial pressure relief valve.
- **You miss out on expanding topics.** When things are calm and good is exactly the time to cover less urgent topics like career growth and feedback on the team.

### What to Do Instead: Reschedule

The solution to canceling 1 on 1s is quite simple: **reschedule instead.**

When you reschedule you're saying: "You're important to me, and while I can't meet right now, I value our time in 1 on 1s enough to find another time to meet soon."

Common valid reasons to reschedule (not cancel):

- One of you is out sick or going home early due to a health concern.
- Your or their schedule has shifted and it's no longer ideal to meet at that time.
- A crisis is unfolding that is urgent to deal with. Rather than being stressed and distracted, reschedule for after the issue is resolved.
- One (or both) of you feel fried. Rather than go through the motions tired, pick up a day or two later.

## Bad Idea #4: Having Group 1 on 1s

**The Reasoning:** "A key topic I want to cover in this meeting also impacts them, so let's have them join our 1 on 1."

**The Result:** Whether it's someone in HR, your boss, a coworker, or peer, bringing anyone else to your 1 on 1s is a terrible idea. It violates the principle of what a 1 on 1 is supposed to be (a private conversation between an employee and their manager). It can be very intimidating to your team member, causing them to be scared and defensive. It also tends to cause long-term damage as it betrays trust.

No matter the situation, it's never a good idea. When you put someone in that situation, they're bound to wonder:

- Am I in trouble? Am I getting fired or laid off? (Especially if the person joining is in HR)
- Is the company in trouble? Am I about to get really bad news? (Especially if the person joining is a founder or executive)
- What is the point of all of this? I guess my 1 on 1 topics are on hold. (For any other guest joining)

Adding someone else completely changes the dynamic. It also sub-communicates that your team member's interests don't matter — they're likely to discard everything they were going to bring, because the trust of the meeting is broken.

### What to Do Instead: Keep 1 on 1s for Just the 2 of You

If someone else is really interested in a subject you plan to cover, your options are:

- Have a separate meeting for the 3 of you (but recognize the gravity that will likely be sensed by your team member)
- Share notes and takeaways with the other person after the meeting
- Ask the other person what they'd like you to ask and ask on their behalf instead of having them present
- Each of you meet with your team member separately, then convene after to compare notes

Some things really are as simple as they sound. Avoiding the awkwardness and discomfort of a group 1 on 1 is as simple as saying, "I don't think that's a good idea. That's not how the meetings are designed to work."

## Bad Idea #5: Rotating Who Someone Has Their 1 on 1s With Regularly

**The Reasoning:** "We really care about our teams, but responsibility is divided. So rather than everyone having 1 manager, we rotate who they have 1 on 1s with. This gives us both a perspective on how the person is doing."

**The Result:** While it sounds really nice and caring, the problem is the constant change prevents any type of depth in the relationship. It kills all momentum in the meetings as there is no continuity from one discussion to the next.

Truly shared management fails when both managers try to do everything:

- Team members generally have shallow relationships with both managers; they never quite get enough time to deepen the relationship.
- A "Mom & Dad dynamic" can develop — team members learn to ask the person most likely to say yes, even if they're not the ideal person to talk with about it.
- Performance is unclear, because you don't know which person's views actually impact your reviews.
- 1 on 1s get repetitive. With constant switching between managers, each manager is out of the loop on the other's conversation. This is frustrating for team members and disincentivizes digging into big issues.
- Things always get lost. Trying to have two managers constantly share everything from their conversations always leads to some loss in translation.

### What to Do Instead: Clearly Defined Roles

You need a primary relationship. For those in matrix organizations, you can absolutely have meetings with others (that's what peer 1 on 1s are for) but you need one person who:

- Builds a deeper, personal relationship with them — who do they go to when they have a personal problem impacting their work?
- Helps with their career growth and will be their primary advocate at compensation, review, and promotion time?
- Reviews the majority of their work and can provide coaching and feedback?

Follow these 3 key rules of thumb:

- **Have 1 primary contact manager** — The team member should know who they come to first and should have more frequent 1 on 1s with their primary manager.
- **Have explicit, specific focus areas everyone understands** — If some management responsibility is divided, make sure all 3 of you understand and agree to that.
- **Sync up with each other semi-regularly** — Use a Peer 1 on 1 between the two managers rather than hoping someone reads and understands your notes.

## Your Action Today: Clean Up Any Bad Ideas in Your 1 on 1s

- **If you're getting a lot of status updates in your 1 on 1s,** start working on getting them another way. Talk to your team about what they feel is the best way for everyone to share status with each other.
- **If you're using the 10/10/10 rule or something similar,** stop. Try to carve out an hour for everyone, and be intentional yet flexible with the time.
- **If you're canceling 1 on 1s often,** start rescheduling instead. If you find yourself canceling or rescheduling a lot, take a hard look at your calendar and make adjustments to a time you're more likely to stick with.
- **If you've ever been tempted to have a group 1 on 1,** don't do it. And if you already have had one, apologize to your team member and promise not to do it again.
- **If you're splitting management responsibilities with someone,** make time in the next week or so to sync up and clearly define how you'll share responsibilities so there is a clear owner of all key areas. Then, share it with your team, so they know what to expect, too.

## Bonus for the Pros: Sharing Is Caring

The best leaders look to teach and help others. Since a pro would know not to do all of the above mistakes, the best thing you can do is help others avoid the same fate.

Go over the further reading on these topics and save the ones you think you're most likely to want to share with others. When the opportunity presents itself, share the appropriate resource with them.

I've lost count of the number of times I've shared blog posts and books with people who needed to learn about a certain topic, and they've been grateful. When you're learning something new or trying to understand the faults in your thinking, these can be transformative.
