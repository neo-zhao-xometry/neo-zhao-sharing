# LH Lessons #2 — The Essential Foundation for Every 1 on 1 Relationship

*From Jason Evanish, Get Lighthouse*

---

There's a lot of different things you can talk about in your 1 on 1s — literally dozens of topics, circumstances, and challenges that all can lead to important, valuable conversations. Yet, there's one topic that you must cover with *everyone*. It is the foundational starting point of every relationship.

## Building Rapport: The First Crucial Step to Great 1 on 1s

Relatedness, trust, comfort, understanding, empathy, affinity, or rapport. Whatever you call it, you need to build connection with your team members.

Think about it: If a friend asked you, "How is your mother doing?" knowing that she's been sick, you'd appreciate the gesture and likely share details. Meanwhile, if a stranger asked you the same thing, you'd politely deflect.

While you're not trying to be best friends with your employees, getting to know them, understanding what's most important to them, and taking a genuine interest in them is important. They want to know you see them as a complete human being, not just a robot outputting code/sales calls/designs/etc.

## Does Building Rapport Really Matter?

**Gallup** found a direct relationship between employee engagement and how approachable an employee feels their manager is. Employees who can approach their manager with any question, or talk to them about non-work-related issues, have dramatically higher odds of engagement. And engagement matters:

- 21% more productivity
- 22% more profitability

In today's world, where many are working remotely and potentially thousands of miles away, it's even harder to tell if your team members are engaged — which makes building strong relationships even more important.

**Google's Project Oxygen** (from their People Analytics team, studying 50,000+ employees) found that one of their key manager survey questions is: "My manager shows consideration for me as a person." The same finding: having foundational trust and the belief that you care about your team is key to your working relationship.

## The Immeasurable Benefits of Rapport

> "So many conflicts at work could be mitigated if you just gave people the benefit of the doubt."
> — Indra Nooyi, former CEO of Pepsi

It is so much easier for you to assume positive intent — and for your team to feel the same about you — if you have that feeling of connection and care as humans.

### Psychological Safety

When your team has psychological safety, they will:

- Participate more actively in meetings
- Have no fears sharing bold and ambitious ideas
- Be okay with sharing something that doesn't work out
- Want to contribute to the team more

And that all starts with **you**. If they don't feel safe with you, they'll never share anything of importance in your 1 on 1s.

### Rapport Is the Foundation for Everything Else

Before you can do anything else as a manager, you need to build rapport. This trust opens the doors to:

- Team members telling you about a problem that affects their work that you wouldn't have known about
- Bold ideas that could blow your team's goals out of the water
- Their openness to your feedback and questions

If your team doesn't trust you and think you care about them, then few of the other tactics will work. They'll tune you out, give one-word answers, and you'll wonder what the point of all of this was.

## Caveat: You Are NOT Their Best Friend

As former Twitter CEO Dick Costolo put it:

> "As a leader, you need to care deeply, deeply about your people while not worrying or really even caring about what they think about you. Managing by trying to be liked is the path to ruin."

As you build rapport, recognize that you want to get to know your people and show you care — but you're not trying to be their friend. This is still a professional relationship.

- Don't become their therapist
- Don't cross into deeply personal, unprofessional territory
- Your 1 on 1 is about *them*, not about them learning a ton about you

## Your Action: Find Their Rhythm

Marissa Mayer, former CEO of Yahoo, learned that most people have something she calls their "Rhythm" — the activity that matters so much to them that resentment sets in when they can't do it. It's different for everyone:

- Being home for dinner with their kids
- Not missing a workout class or meditation time
- Making a family sporting event
- A weekly social gathering with friends

Add this question to all your 1 on 1 agendas:

> "What's something you do on a weekly or monthly basis that makes you really happy, and it's a much tougher week if you don't get to do it? I want to work with you to try to make sure you don't miss it."

This both builds rapport *and* gives you crucial information about what matters most to each person. Never assume you already know what their rhythm is, and never assume it stays the same — lives and circumstances change.
