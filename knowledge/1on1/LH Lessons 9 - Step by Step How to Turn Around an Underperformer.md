# LH Lessons #9 — Step by Step How to Turn Around an Underperformer

*From Jason Evanish, Get Lighthouse*

---

## Great Managers Make the Effort

The easiest thing in the world to do as a manager is to complain about the weaker people on your team. Maybe you inherited them, they transferred to your team, or they're not what you thought they'd be when you hired them.

Regardless, if they're on your team, you should make the effort to help them succeed.

Ultimately their performance is on them to meet minimal standards and put in effort to get better. However, the work you put in to truly help them improve can make all the difference between an awkward, drawn-out process to terminate them, and helping them flip to thriving.

---

## Lesson #9: Step-by-Step How to Turn Around an Underperforming Team Member

It's expensive to replace an employee — in the cost of a tight compensation market and the time you and your team spend interviewing:

- Phone screen 25 candidates, 30 minutes each = 12.5 hours for you
- Another team member screens your 10 favorites, 30 minutes each = 5 hours for them
- 4 finalists come on site and meet with you and 4 other team members, each for an hour = 4 hours for you, 16 hours for your team
- Top 2 you work on offers and follow-ups = 4 more hours for you

That conservative estimate adds up to **20+ hours for you, and 21+ hours for your team.** Wouldn't you rather spend those hours on making progress on your team's goals?

*Note: If you're looking to apply formal Performance Improvement Plans by the legal definition, check with your HR/People department first. This is not legal advice — it's making your best effort as a leader, which is often the last step before working with HR on a formal process, and gives you a detailed record of your efforts.*

---

## Step 1) Make a List of Issues

You can't fix a vague notion. You can fix when someone interrupts others in meetings, and talks over colleagues.

Make a list so that you have a foundation for your conversation. Ask yourself:

- What are recent examples of times they disappointed?
- How have they let the team down? How did it affect others?
- What specific tasks did they deliver poor work on?
- What *specifically* was wrong with their work?
- What are they doing that your best people do not?
- What are they not doing that your best people are doing?

Don't short-change this step. If anything, try to write what feels like too much.

---

## Step 2) Find the Patterns

Now that you've built a list of things that are issues, look for the patterns.

Dumping 20 complaints on someone would overwhelm them. However, if you instead come to them with 3–4 common issues you find they have often, and then give 4–5 strong examples of it happening with how it affects everyone, it can work well.

Use your pattern matching skills to think about how, say, a lack of attention to detail manifests itself across a bunch of different tasks. Chances are, there are a few root issues that tie all their problems together.

---

## Step 3) Put It on Your Private 1 on 1 Agenda

As discussed in Lesson 8, your 1 on 1s are the perfect time to work on these issues — because of the length of time you have to meet, the private nature, and the focus on them in the meetings.

With the patterns you've identified with examples and impacts noted, add them to your next 1 on 1 with the team member.

**Important:** Make them part of notes just for you. You don't want to freak them out, or take your notes out of context before you have the chance to discuss them.

When you discuss the issues, it should be the first time they see or hear them. As Paul English, founder of Kayak, says, you should always talk about an issue before it's in writing.

This is because so much more can happen in a live discussion:

- You can read their body language: Do they understand? Are they confused?
- They can understand your tone: Are you serious about this?
- You can ask each other questions: Are your patterns clear to them? Do they agree?

You also get their first reaction — if this is the first time they hear your feedback, you can get their real reaction instead of them potentially derailing the discussion with excuses.

---

## Step 4) Start Your 1 on 1 Talking About What They Want & How They're Doing

Yes, even with your underperformers, you still want to follow the Listen step of Prepare-Listen-Act.

As a manager, you only know a small fraction of the world your employees are living in. By starting the 1 on 1 with what's on their mind, you could find out something really important that explains their poor performance:

- They were severely over-extended and specifically wanted help so they could do better work.
- They had a personal incident that they'd reported to the police, but had not been able to tell you yet.
- A close family member had died and while they had returned to work, it was still deeply affecting them.
- A personal illness was affecting their ability to work, and they knew they were struggling.
- They hated their job and wanted to quit or find a better fitting role.

Assuming instead that there's not such an issue, you then move forward with the performance discussion.

---

## Step 5) Ask Good Questions to Get Them to Open Up

You want to hear their side of the story. Some possible questions:

- How are you doing?
- How has your workload felt recently? Too much, not enough, or okay?
- How do you think [problem project] is going?
- What do you find most challenging in your role? How have you tried to handle that?
- How would you rate your work on the last [project/task/etc]? Why that rating?
- What do you enjoy least in your role? What do you enjoy most? Why?

All of this is to give you context. Even if you disagree with everything they say, at least you'll know they don't see what you do — which is exactly why you prepared all those examples.

---

## Step 6) Transition to Your Feedback and Coaching

Now that you've heard them out, you can move into the specific issues, patterns, and examples you've noted. You may want to tie it to something they said: "I'm glad you've brought this up, I wanted to talk to you about..."

Typically, when you bring up a pattern, they'll become defensive or ask for an example. This is what you prepared for.

Since you have pulled together examples with notes on the impact they had, you can walk them through everything. And by having multiple examples prepared, you ensure they realize this is an ongoing issue, not an isolated incident.

Use active listening skills by asking your team member to summarize to you what they heard. This confirms they understand what you're trying to get across.

Clarify with them if you feel they missed anything you wanted to get across on the issues, and the stakes involved — the latter is just as important, as it needs to be clear to them that this isn't friendly feedback, it's things that must change.

---

## Step 7) Create Concrete Next Steps

A conversation about performance is great, but it must be actionable. This is where many managers make their biggest mistake — they have a vague, general conversation about someone needing to improve, and then end the meeting.

The team member knows they need to improve, but their idea of what to do and the manager's are totally different. Not surprisingly, they don't get better.

Set clear expectations on what they should do differently. Ask yourself these questions as you're setting next steps:

- **What exactly should they stop, or start doing?** Like starting to use a spell-checker before submitting work for review.
- **How will you determine if things are getting better?** Will you be checking with certain colleagues? Is there a metric you will monitor?
- **What action do they need to take to make amends?** If you expect them to apologize to a colleague, get specific about when, where, and how.
- **Who will be offering further help?** Can they pair with a colleague who will help teach them? Will you be reviewing their work?
- **Where can they reference more help or information?** If they're trying to improve a skill, point them in the right direction.
- **How will you be evaluating their work in this area?** Make it clear what you'll be looking for and how you'll judge success and failure.
- **When will you be talking with them again about this?** By setting clear expectations on when you'll continue the conversation, you create urgency to act immediately.

---

## Step 8) Put It in Writing

Equally important, you need to put this all in writing. Write down next steps so you can reference them next time, and send them a follow-up email that recaps what you talked about and the expected next steps you'll be looking for.

Doing this leaves zero doubt of your expectations.

This gives you clear documentation of your process: Not only do you have your 1 on 1 notes, but you have an email with timestamps showing how you put in effort and clearly communicated issues and expected changes. If this doesn't succeed, you can prove to HR and anyone else you need to convince about letting them go.

---

## Step 9) Continue the Conversation

People do not turn around in a day, nor even a week. It's an ongoing effort.

In the case of a team member you want to improve, meet more often — try having weekly 1 on 1s, even if that's uncommon for you. This creates a tight feedback loop between talking about an issue, setting next steps, and checking back in how they're doing.

In these follow-up conversations, ask yourself:

- Are they completing the action items and meeting the expectations set from last meeting?
- What feedback do I need to provide based on their recent performance versus the patterns we're trying to improve?
- What can I praise that they've improved? (Remember Lesson 8: motivate them to keep getting better!)

By considering those 3 things, you should start to see them turn around. The positive momentum will start to make the issues disappear, and they'll be performing up to your standard.

Or, in some unfortunate cases, you'll find it's a broken record — you'll be talking about the same issues again and again. Because you've been sending follow-ups and preparing well, you'll have all the records you need to move on from them.

---

## Your Action Today: Start This Process for an Underperformer, or Save It for Later

Following this approach, you'll be in good company, as one leader told us:

> "I had 2 team members that struggled in different ways, and I was able to use 1:1s to spot patterns of concern and then address in a clear manner, creating action items for improvement, and follow up in more frequent 1:1s documenting improvement.
>
> Through this, both team members improved and have excelled."

They were actually about to fire those people when they started this approach, but a year later those people were not only still at the company but thriving — with one even up for promotion.

Maybe the "lost cause" or "dead weight" on your team just needs a little coaching and structured feedback. You won't know unless you try.
