# LH Lessons #6 — How to Get Buy-In from Your Team and Leverage Your 1 on 1s

*From Jason Evanish, Get Lighthouse*

---

Have you ever told your team about a change, or new initiative, and seen them roll their eyes or been met by unexpected resistance? Chances are, at some point, you have.

Not every change will be popular. Nor will they all be clear and obvious to everyone from the start. Yet, getting buy-in and support for the direction your team or company is going is one of the essential jobs in leading your team.

That's why your 1 on 1s are a key tool in making it happen.

---

## Lesson #6: How to Get Buy-in with 1 on 1s + the First Follower

This lesson starts with a concept from entrepreneur Derek Sivers, whose "How to Start a Movement" talk (a man dancing on a hillside) captures the essence of what it means to lead and build momentum.

---

## Appreciate Your First Follower

> "It takes guts to be a First Follower! You stand out and brave ridicule, yourself. Being a First Follower is an under-appreciated form of leadership.
>
> The First Follower transforms a lone nut into a leader. If the leader is the flint, the First Follower is the spark that makes the fire." — Derek Sivers

If you've ever made a suggestion and heard crickets in a meeting as no one supported you, you know how important support from others is. No piece of support is more important than the first, brave soul who agrees before everyone else does.

---

## It's About Both of You

> "This is key. You must be easy to follow! … Notice the leader embraces [the First Follower] as an equal, so it's not about the leader anymore — it's about them, plural." — Derek Sivers

Whether the idea or initiative was yours from the start or assigned to you from a superior, keep in mind the importance of some flexibility. The more you can take some input and listen to your team to help shape it, the more success you'll have getting them on board versus rigidly dictating to them.

Think about what the core of what you're doing is: what is the outcome you're trying to get? What must happen versus can be flexible in how it's done? The flexibility represents opportunity for your First Follower (and many future ones) to buy in and go along with it.

---

## Start Small

The biggest mistake of many leaders is to make an important decision on their own, and then present it to a large group without any input. This is a recipe for disaster.

If you don't take the time to ask for input from those you're trying to lead, you're unlikely to know what they're most concerned or excited about. Tell them what you're thinking about, or a change that's coming, and pay attention to their reactions, questions, and concerns.

- Knowing your team's concerns will help you overcome and anticipate objections that could come in a larger setting.
- Knowing what excites them will help you tailor presenting your idea to what will motivate and persuade your team most.
- Without your team's input, you miss out on making your movement more successful by tweaking things based on their feedback.

---

## Leverage Your 1 on 1s

If you're starting a movement, or need to make a significant change at work, using your 1 on 1s to get buy-in and input is a powerful way to ensure success, and find your First Follower.

The privacy of the meeting can lead to valuable candor, and ensure you get a diversity of ideas instead of groupthink in a public setting. Instead of getting only the first idea from the loudest person on your team in a group meeting, you hear everyone's input individually in a 1 on 1.

As serial entrepreneur and investor Michael Wolfe describes, imagine the CEO tells you Monday that they're concerned about slowing revenue growth and wants a new plan by next Monday. Your week might then look like this: you share the problem with your team, work it through in your 1 on 1s, then come prepared the following Monday with a new plan. And you:

- Did not have to scramble — you used meeting time you already had scheduled.
- Did not panic anyone — no last-minute emergency meetings.
- Gave your team time to generate ideas and contribute.
- Got a pulse on how your team is feeling and soothed concerns they may have.
- Came into the following Monday meeting with a plan and insight on team morale.

> "This is what rapid and efficient decision-making looks like. This is what keeps companies marching forward week after week, never getting bogged down by indecision."

---

## How Do You Propel It into a Movement?

> "Now here come 2 more, then 3 more. Now we've got momentum. This is the tipping point! Now we've got a movement!" — Derek Sivers

While it's important to get that First Follower, your work isn't done once you have them. Things don't tip until there are a few people involved.

The first follower isn't always who you expect; the first person you speak with may have more questions, concerns, or reticence than others. It's only once you've talked to a few people that you find the person who is the right balance of interested, capable, and ready to go. You'll also find out what is a one-off concern and what your whole team is actually pushing back on.

---

## Don't Rely on the Teacher's Pet

One of the easy mistakes to make as a leader is to fall into a rut — always going to the same go-to team member to be your First Follower. Be careful. It can create a series of problems:

- **They may not represent the team** — what they think may be different than their colleagues.
- **The team may resent them** — your favored team member may appear to be a "teacher's pet" if they receive preferential treatment.
- **Others feel marginalized** — if you're always soliciting feedback from certain people, others may feel their input doesn't matter.

Instead, consider who the best people on your team would be for each situation. If you can't talk to everyone, involving the people most representative, most qualified, or most impacted by the change will be your best way to avoid potential pitfalls and politics.

---

## Your Action Today: Get More Buy-In for an Upcoming or Recent Change

Take a moment and think about a change that is coming soon, or just recently happened. Now is your chance to practice getting buy-in from your team.

Ask yourself:

- **Who would be most interested in this?** Plan to take them aside and talk to them to see if your instinct was right and that you understand their motivations.
- **Who would be the biggest critic or skeptic?** Plan to talk to them to listen and learn their concerns. Work with them to see how you can help them understand why this is important, or find subtle tweaks based on their skepticism.
- **What areas of this are rigid and which are flexible?** Identify areas where you can take input that can make them feel heard and some ownership in the project, and where you cannot.

### Buy-In Questions for Your 1 on 1

Open up your 1 on 1 notes and add these questions for handy reference:

- Is any part of this confusing or unclear? What can I clarify for you?
- What's your biggest concern about this?
- What's most interesting or exciting about this to you?
- How do you feel about this change?
- If you could change or tweak anything about this, what would you want to change? What makes that important to you?
- How do you think the rest of the team will take this news?

By asking these questions, good follow-ups, and practicing your Active Listening skills from Lesson 4, you'll build the buy-in and support you need.

---

## Bonus for the Pros: How Can You Be a Better First Follower?

As important as your own initiatives can be, being a great follower yourself is a skill that great leaders must learn to master, too.

When you know how to be a great follower, you become an asset to any group or organization you're a part of. You also build allies who may come to your aid when you need help in the future.

Take a look around your workplace today. What initiatives do your peers and your boss have that they may be seeking a first follower for?

To find the right one to be a first follower for, ask yourself:

- **Is this something you can help out with?** Focus on areas where you can add real value and have the time and ability to be a good follower.
- **What questions would you have as a skeptic?** Your questions could help the leader improve their project.
- **Who else could you bring on board?** Few things are as valuable as a first follower who can get others excited and identify the right person for the task.
- **What's interesting or exciting about this?** Bringing another perspective on what's valuable can help the leader with their pitch.
