## Lesson 12: Manager Q&A — You Asked, We Answered

---

### 1) "How does the location of a 1 on 1 impact it? Should I have 1 on 1s in different places depending on the topics covered?"

Having a routine place to have your 1 on 1s can work great. It becomes comfortable, familiar, and feels safe for both of you. I know managers who have used each of the following:

- A preferred conference room far away from the team work area for extra comfort and privacy.
- Going for a walk outside to get sun and fresh air while you talk.
- Going to a nearby park, or the roof of the building to relax and break up the day.

Iterate on what feels most comfortable for your 1 on 1s. At the same time, it can help to mix up locations depending on your agenda and topics:

- **When giving key feedback about their performance,** use a conference room at work to set a more serious, professional tone.
- **When you want a more rapport-focused 1 on 1,** going outside or for a walk helps relax the meeting.
- **Need to talk about a coworker and want to ensure it's private?** Head to a coffee shop or grab lunch at a place the rest of the company doesn't go to.

And if you're remote, consider:

- Use Zoom with video on in a quiet part of your home or coworking space for giving important feedback.
- Go for a walk and encourage your team member to as well, to have a less formal, rapport-focused 1 on 1.

### 2) "What advice do you have for taking notes in 1 on 1s? I'm having a hard time doing so while we meet."

A few methods that can help managers with their 1 on 1 note-taking:

- **The key line:** "That sounds important to you. Let me write that down." Use this any time you're in the middle of a 1 on 1 and want to be sure you don't miss what they said. Saying this line is always well received.
- **The Last 5 Minutes Method:** If you don't like taking notes as you go, save 5 minutes at the end of your 1 on 1 to recap what you think you heard and take notes. Let your team member clarify anything they feel you missed.
- **Schedule 10 minutes after:** Just like you schedule your 1 on 1s, schedule time to take notes right after, while it's still fresh in your mind.

### 3) "What advice do you have for specifically 1 on 1s with remote employees?"

Remote 1 on 1s are largely the same as other 1 on 1s. You should still talk about their careers, build rapport, give them coaching and feedback, and do everything we talked about in this program.

However, there are a few additional things to do specifically for your remote team members:

- Check in on how their work setup is and make sure they can be productive and focused.
- Make sure they have good habits to recharge and can effectively separate work and personal life.
- Give extra effort and attention to making sure they feel in the loop, as they may miss out on some company announcements, in-office conversations, etc.

### 4) "Do you have a favorite coaching framework (OSKAR, GROW, etc.) you recommend to use in 1 on 1s?"

I'm a fan of using the right tool for the right job. As our lessons showed, there are different approaches to use for different situations.

Before starting any framework, take time not just to understand what the framework is, but also when is the right time to use it. For instance, the Prepare-Listen-Act framework we talked about for feedback is best used when you have a pattern of problems to fix that you want your team member to work on.

Meanwhile, OSKAR seems better suited for when a team member comes to you saying "I'd like your coaching on X" — that then makes it easier to follow the Outcome and Affirm steps. Otherwise, I think outcome comes later in the discussion (not first) and Affirm would be patronizing if they just found out about the issue.

My recommendation: use what you feel would be the right tool for the situation, then observe how it went and ask your team. Keep what works for you, iterate or discard what does not.

### 5) "I am doing 1 on 1s with someone that is going to leave my team, but wants to continue having our 1 on 1s. Should we continue doing them?"

Your dynamic is about to shift so be mindful and intentional about that. A few aspects to consider:

- **Who is their new manager?** Make sure you hand off core responsibilities to that manager, so you avoid the mistake from Lesson 11 where someone has 2 managers and isn't sure who to go to for what.
- **Agree on what you'll cover in your new 1 on 1s.** This should be agreed on by you and them, as well as letting their new manager know. Done right, it can be great to have two different teams that work together often on the same page. Recognize that now you're having **Peer 1 on 1s** — they have a different purpose and goal.
- **Dial back the frequency a bit if you met weekly.** Every 2–4 weeks should be plenty for a peer 1 on 1, with monthly being enough if your work doesn't overlap a lot.

If you have a great working relationship with someone and they leave your team, peer 1 on 1s are a great way to maintain the work friendship and help two teams work better together. The key is to adjust the frequency and coordinate with their manager, so it's clear how your dynamic is shifting.

### 6) "If you have a reserved team member who doesn't feel comfortable sharing much about themselves, how do you build rapport with them?"

There are absolutely people who prefer to be more private, and that's okay. There are literally hundreds of ways to build rapport, and many of them leave plenty of privacy for the person you're speaking with.

Look for hints to ask about things, such as:

- Something they wear (like a band shirt, or being more dressed up one day because they have a fun event after work)
- A memento or item on their desk or in the Zoom background you can ask about
- A book they've been reading you see laying out, or a podcast or show they enjoy

There are definitely people who are some mix of shy, private, and/or introverted. While it may sometimes feel tougher to engage them, the extra effort is worth it.

You may be surprised how, if you strike the right topic, they'll light up with more engagement and energy. If they're private, focus on "safer" topics like work interests, hobbies, and things they seem to actively be displaying around them.

### 7) "How do you measure the success of a 1 on 1? I've read some articles that say the 1:1 is successful if the employee talks for 50% or more of the time. Is that a good measure?"

Unfortunately, there is not an objective measure for 1 on 1s that you can easily quantify. Maybe you can look for improvements in your scores on a 360 review, but that's a lagging indicator and can change for other reasons, too.

Instead, it should be evident through a few signs you'll see for yourself:

- **Yes, them talking more is a great sign,** especially if they weren't talking before. That means they're opening up and trusting you more.
- **Are they more engaged and effective in their work?** 1 on 1s are supposed to improve the quality of their work and your relationship.
- **Do they listen to you more?** Are they more open to your feedback, ideas, and questions? These are more signs of progress on your relationship, which is the core goal of your 1 on 1s.
- **Do you look forward to the meetings now instead of seeing them as a chore?** If things are working well in the meetings, you should feel a boost in confidence in both running the meetings and that you'll be doing something valuable in them.

In general, you'll know they're successful when other things start trending in a better direction — whether that's the direct focus (like a performance issue you went over) or things that come as a result (more trust and understanding).
